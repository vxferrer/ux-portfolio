Lumix ‚Äî Designing financial clarity through emotional regulation

How might we help young professionals who earn enough but still feel financially anxious? We designed a behavioral fintech app that connects real bank data with emotional awareness ‚Äî replacing overwhelm with clarity.

Fintech Finance Wellness
4 teammates Fintech / Wellness UX Research Product Design Mobile App 10 days
OVERVIEW

Lumix is a behavioral fintech concept designed during a 10-day design sprint at Ironhack. The brief was open: identify a real problem and design a mobile product from scratch ‚Äî from research to hi-fi prototype.

Our team of four chose to explore the intersection of personal finance and mental health after noticing that most financial tools on the market focus on numbers and dashboards, while the real barrier for many users is emotional ‚Äî anxiety, guilt, and avoidance.

My role: I led research synthesis, proposed the core product concept (emotional calendar, daily challenges, and the Discover section), defined the information architecture, and designed key interaction flows and UI screens in Figma. I also shaped the product's emotional tone ‚Äî friendly language, no technical jargon, no punitive feedback.

THE CHALLENGE

We started with a hypothesis: young professionals with stable income shouldn't feel financially stressed ‚Äî but they do.

Secondary research confirmed the scale of the problem: 31% of young adults (20‚Äì34) in Spain are at risk of depression or anxiety due to economic insecurity. 58% feel anxiety when they receive a spending notification. And 64% spend beyond their means and feel guilty about it.

The data pointed to a paradox: having income doesn't guarantee feeling at peace. The problem isn't financial literacy alone ‚Äî it's emotional. And the tools that exist today were making it worse, not better.

RESEARCH & DISCOVERY

We ran a multi-method research approach to understand not just what people do with money, but how they feel about it:

  1. Quantitative survey ‚Äî 85 respondents, active workers aged 22‚Äì35. Focused on financial stress frequency, emotional triggers, tool usage, and savings behavior.
  2. Expert interview ‚Äî A clinical psychologist specializing in the emotional relationship with money. This was critical to understanding the behavioral patterns behind financial avoidance.
  3. Secondary research ‚Äî Studies on financial stress among Spanish youth, mental health data, and behavioral finance patterns.

KEY INSIGHTS

The research revealed four insights that fundamentally shaped our product direction:

  1. Anxiety is persistent, not situational. 46% feel financial stress frequently. Only 5.8% never feel anxiety about money. Even with stable jobs, the stress doesn't disappear ‚Äî it's structural, not temporary.
  2. The future is the biggest emotional trigger. 18.5% named "thinking about the future" as their primary source of financial distress ‚Äî more than unexpected expenses (11.4%) or checking their balance (8.5%). The opportunity: transform uncertainty into predictability.
  3. People want to save but can't sustain the habit. 56% associate saving with peace of mind, but a third can't maintain it. 16.7% abandon financial apps because they don't help change behavior. Users don't need more data ‚Äî they need behavioral support.
  4. Financial apps fail emotionally. Only 3.9% use dedicated budgeting apps. 15.3% say seeing numbers triggers anxiety. 8.3% say apps make them feel guilty. 36% have never used a financial app because they're afraid of facing their own numbers. The problem isn't the concept of managing money ‚Äî it's how it's presented.
EXPERT PERSPECTIVE

The psychologist interview added a critical layer: financial anxiety is an identity threat, not just a budget problem. Money activates shame, low self-esteem, and fear because people experience it as a measure of their personal worth and adult capability.

Key takeaways for design: avoidance (not checking balances) is a self-protection strategy, not laziness. Saving is an emotional skill, not just a rational one. And financial management can become a form of self-care ‚Äî but only if the experience feels safe, supportive, and non-judgmental.

This reframed our entire approach. We weren't designing a budgeting tool ‚Äî we were designing for emotional regulation.

DEFINING THE PROBLEM

We synthesized the research into a user persona and a clear problem statement:

Ver√≥nica, 30. Primary school teacher in Valencia. Stable job, can pay bills and save a bit each month. But she lives with constant financial pressure. She's tried budgeting apps but abandons them because they create more stress than they solve. She checks her banking app daily ‚Äî she's not sure if it's control or anxiety. She doesn't want to be rich. She just wants to feel like she's making progress.

Problem statement: Young professionals with stable employment experience financial anxiety not from lack of income, but from lack of predictability. They need a structured, emotionally intelligent way to understand their money ‚Äî to transform income into a real sense of security.

PRODUCT STRATEGY

Instead of building another dashboard-heavy finance app, we defined four product pillars directly derived from our research:

  1. Clarity ‚Äî Simplified financial overviews that reduce cognitive load instead of adding to it. No complex graphs, no jargon.
  2. Automation ‚Äî Bank account connection with automatic categorization, so users don't need to manually track every expense.
  3. Projection ‚Äî Predictive guidance that transforms future uncertainty into visible progress. Savings goals with clear timelines.
  4. Safety ‚Äî Emotionally intelligent feedback, supportive language, and a companion that makes facing financial data feel like self-care rather than punishment.
FEATURE PRIORITIZATION

We used the MoSCoW method and an impact/effort matrix to define what would make it into our MVP. The core principle was deliberate restraint: what we leave out matters as much as what we include.

Must have: Emotional + financial onboarding, clean home screen, weekly calendar with mood tracking, savings goal visualization, Discover section (articles, podcasts, meditations), and the AI companion chatbot.

Deliberately excluded: Complex dashboards, technical graphs, data tables, investment planning, and professional-grade financial tools. If it could trigger anxiety or cognitive overload, it didn't make the cut.

INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE

The app structure was designed around five core areas, each mapped to a specific user need identified in research:

  1. Home ‚Äî The emotional anchor. Daily challenge, mood check-in, weekly summary. Designed to be the first thing users see ‚Äî calm, personal, actionable.
  2. Management ‚Äî Monthly financial overview with categorized expenses and income. Simplified visualization, no complex charts.
  3. Savings ‚Äî One active goal at a time (to reduce decision fatigue), with clear progress and predictive timeline.
  4. Discover ‚Äî Educational content: articles, podcasts, meditations, quizzes. Built to improve financial literacy without intimidation.
  5. Lumix ‚Äî The AI companion. A conversational assistant that translates bank data into friendly, actionable advice. Designed as a mascot to lower the psychological barrier of facing financial information.

We mapped detailed user flows for each section: app onboarding, financial dashboard navigation, chatbot consultation, content discovery, and profile management.

KEY DESIGN DECISIONS

Every design choice was mapped back to a research insight. Here are the most impactful ones:

  1. Emotional calendar on the home screen. Our psychologist told us that being present with emotions while facing financial data is key to regulation. The daily mood check-in connects how users feel to what they spend ‚Äî making patterns visible without judgment.
  2. Daily challenges instead of to-do lists. Saving is an emotional skill, not a rational one. Micro-challenges ("Do you really need that purchase?") build habits through small wins, not pressure.
  3. One savings goal at a time. Research showed decision fatigue increases anxiety. By limiting active goals, we reduce cognitive load and increase perceived progress.
  4. A mascot as the AI interface. 36% of users avoid financial apps because facing numbers is emotionally painful. A friendly character creates a psychologically safe layer between the user and their data.
  5. Discover section with curated content. Only 19% of Spanish people have high financial literacy. Instead of overwhelming users with tools, we offer digestible content (articles, podcasts, meditations) to build knowledge gradually.
THE PROTOTYPE

We built a hi-fi interactive prototype in Figma covering the complete user journey: onboarding, home with emotional calendar, financial overview, savings tracking, content discovery, and the AI companion chatbot.

FINANCIAL CLARITY

The financial overview replaces dense dashboards with a simplified monthly summary. Expenses are shown as categorized bubbles ‚Äî visually light, easy to scan. No tables, no overwhelming numbers. The goal: users should understand their situation in under 5 seconds.

CONVERSATIONAL SUPPORT

The AI companion "Lumix" translates financial data into clear, friendly recommendations. When a user asks "Can I afford this?", the chatbot doesn't just show numbers ‚Äî it provides context, reassurance, and actionable next steps. The tone is warm, never punitive.

EDUCATION & GUIDANCE

The Discover section offers articles, podcasts, and meditations ‚Äî not as filler content, but as a core product feature. Each piece is designed to build financial literacy through micro-habits and mindset shifts, making the user's relationship with money healthier over time.

MY ROLE IN DEPTH

In a team of four, I took ownership of several key areas:

  1. Core concept ideation. I proposed the central ideas that defined the product: the emotional calendar with daily mood tracking, the daily financial challenges, the Discover section (articles, podcasts, meditations), and the decision to make the AI chatbot a mascot character.
  2. Research synthesis. I led the analysis of our 85-person survey and helped extract the four key insights that shaped the product direction. I also contributed to defining the user persona's personality traits and pain points.
  3. Information architecture. I designed the app structure, navigation system, and the onboarding flow ‚Äî including the emotional + financial initial setup.
  4. Product tone. I pushed for friendly language with subtle humor, no technical jargon, and non-punitive feedback throughout the experience. This came directly from the psychologist's insight that financial tools need to protect user dignity.
  5. UI design. I designed key screens including the dashboard, savings section, and Discover section in Figma.
KEY LEARNINGS

Financial products don't just communicate data ‚Äî they communicate safety. The biggest shift in this project was understanding that the barrier to financial wellness isn't knowledge or tools. It's emotional. People avoid their finances because facing numbers feels like facing personal failure.

Designing for emotional regulation means simplicity increases perceived control, tone influences behavioral consistency, and reducing cognitive load directly impacts trust. What you deliberately exclude from the product matters as much as what you include.

Talking to a psychologist was a turning point. It shifted our perspective from "how do we show financial data better" to "how do we make people feel safe enough to engage with their own finances." That reframe changed everything.

NEXT STEPS

If I continued this project, I would focus on:

  1. Usability testing to validate whether the emotional calendar and daily challenges actually reduce financial anxiety over time.
  2. Behavioral metrics ‚Äî measuring session frequency, mood tracking consistency, savings goal completion rate, and whether users stop avoiding their financial data.
  3. Accessibility audit ‚Äî ensuring the product works for users with different cognitive and emotional needs.
  4. Content strategy ‚Äî developing a sustainable pipeline for the Discover section with expert-backed financial literacy content.
