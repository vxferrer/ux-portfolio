THE CHALLENGE

Airline apps are high-stakes and high-cognitive-load environments: users need to find critical information fast, complete tasks confidently, and understand what the system is doing at all times.

In the original Korean Air app, multiple UI blocks compete for attention, CTAs are unclear, and the hierarchy doesn‚Äôt reflect user intent. The result is a home screen that feels ‚Äúbusy‚Äù and workflows that require extra effort to understand.

Goal: reduce cognitive overload, clarify primary actions (e.g., trips and reservations), and create a consistent UI system that can scale.

HEURISTIC AUDIT

I ran a heuristic evaluation to identify usability issues, assess severity, and prioritize fixes based on impact and ease of implementation. Key findings clustered into a few recurring themes:

  1. Unclear primary CTA: ‚ÄúContinue‚Äù doesn‚Äôt communicate the next step clearly.
  2. Low visual hierarchy: many blocks compete; everything feels equally important.
  3. Inconsistent language and copy: mixed languages reduce cohesion and trust.
  4. Information without context: e.g., miles appear without explanation or guidance.
  5. Help not embedded: lack of microcopy/tooltips for complex actions.

REDESIGN STRATEGY

Instead of redesigning screens ‚Äúby taste‚Äù, I reframed the UI around task priority and decision clarity. The strategy focused on three pillars:

  1. Task-first hierarchy ‚Üí design the home around what users need to do (not what the brand wants to show).
  2. Clarity of actions ‚Üí explicit CTAs and predictable next steps, with supportive microcopy.
  3. System consistency ‚Üí reusable typography, spacing and components to scale without drift.
KEY DESIGN MOVES

Here are the most impactful design decisions mapped to the problems found:

  1. Explicit logged-in state: added a clear welcome header to confirm status and personalize the experience.
  2. Primary CTA clarity: separated ‚ÄúContinue search‚Äù and ‚ÄúNew search‚Äù to restore user control.
  3. Information hierarchy: reduced competing blocks; grouped content into clear categories.
  4. Progress + feedback: introduced clearer flow steps for ‚ÄúAdd reservation‚Äù and reduced form ambiguity.
  5. Contextual help: added short descriptions where users typically hesitate (documents, baggage, alerts).
VISUAL SYSTEM

To prevent inconsistencies and make the redesign scalable, I created lightweight UI guidelines: spacing scale, typography hierarchy, iconography and a refined blue palette aligned with the brand.

  1. Typography: consistent hierarchy for headings/body to improve scanability.
  2. Spacing: 8/16/24/32px rhythm for predictable layouts.
  3. Icons: unified stroke and style for navigation and actions.
  4. Color: structured blues for states, emphasis, and readability.

OUTCOME & NEXT STEPS

This redesign focuses on clarity and trust: fewer competing elements, more explicit actions, and a scalable system. If I continued the project, I would validate improvements with usability testing and measurable product metrics: task completion rate (Add reservation), time on task, and drop-offs on key flows.

Key learning: In complex products (travel, banking, fintech), UX issues are often structural ‚Äî hierarchy, system feedback and clarity matter as much as visual polish.
