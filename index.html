Museek ‚Äî Designing behavioral engagement through gamification

A mobile product concept that transforms traditional museum visits into interactive, challenge-based experiences to increase local cultural engagement among young adults.

3 teammates Leisure / Culture UX Research App 8 days
THE CHALLENGE

While young adults actively consume cultural experiences when traveling, they rarely visit museums in their own cities. Our research revealed a behavioral gap between cultural interest and local participation.

Through 5 qualitative interviews (ages 25‚Äì35, urban and rural), we identified three consistent patterns:

  1. Museums are perceived as passive and static experiences.
  2. Cultural events lack discoverability and clarity.
  3. Social influence and novelty strongly drive decision-making.

This reframed the opportunity from ‚Äúpromoting museums‚Äù to redesigning the experience to create intrinsic motivation and emotional engagement.

PRODUCT STRATEGY

Instead of designing another event listing app, we focused on solving two structural friction points:

  1. Discovery ‚Äî Reducing cognitive effort when finding relevant cultural activities.
  2. Engagement ‚Äî Transforming passive visits into interactive, goal-driven experiences.

Inspired by escape-room mechanics, we introduced a challenge-based system to increase perceived value, session duration and social motivation.

The goal was not gamification for aesthetics, but as a behavioral nudge to drive participation.

INTERACTION DESIGN DECISIONS

To ensure clarity and reduce friction, key design decisions included:

  1. Minimizing onboarding steps to reduce entry friction.
  2. Using progressive disclosure to avoid cognitive overload.
  3. Structuring navigation around challenges instead of static listings.
  4. Designing clear visual hierarchy to support task completion.

These decisions aimed to balance excitement with usability ‚Äî ensuring engagement without sacrificing clarity.

MY ROLE

I led user interviews, synthesized research insights into opportunity areas, and defined the primary user flow. I designed low-fidelity wireframes and developed the high-fidelity interactive prototype in Figma.

VALIDATION & LEARNINGS

During usability testing, users responded positively to the challenge-based concept, highlighting increased perceived excitement and clarity of purpose.

However, early testing revealed confusion around progression mechanics. We iterated by simplifying the task flow and clarifying reward feedback.

Key learning: Gamification increases engagement only when supported by strong information architecture and clear feedback systems.

In future iterations, I would validate behavioral impact through measurable metrics such as completion rate, session duration and return visits.

Korean Air ‚Äî Redesigning clarity and trust in a high-cognitive-load app

Concept redesign based on a heuristic audit to reduce cognitive overload, clarify primary actions, and establish a scalable visual system for a travel product.

Solo project Travel Heuristics / Redesign App Mobile 3 days
THE CHALLENGE

Airline apps are high-stakes and high-cognitive-load environments: users need to find critical information fast, complete tasks confidently, and understand what the system is doing at all times.

In the original Korean Air app, multiple UI blocks compete for attention, CTAs are unclear, and the hierarchy doesn‚Äôt reflect user intent. The result is a home screen that feels ‚Äúbusy‚Äù and workflows that require extra effort to understand.

Goal: reduce cognitive overload, clarify primary actions (e.g., trips and reservations), and create a consistent UI system that can scale.

HEURISTIC AUDIT

I ran a heuristic evaluation to identify usability issues, assess severity, and prioritize fixes based on impact and ease of implementation. Key findings clustered into a few recurring themes:

  1. Unclear primary CTA: ‚ÄúContinue‚Äù doesn‚Äôt communicate the next step clearly.
  2. Low visual hierarchy: many blocks compete; everything feels equally important.
  3. Inconsistent language and copy: mixed languages reduce cohesion and trust.
  4. Information without context: e.g., miles appear without explanation or guidance.
  5. Help not embedded: lack of microcopy/tooltips for complex actions.
üì∑ Imagen 2 del case studyRecomendado: 1600√ó1000px
REDESIGN STRATEGY

Instead of redesigning screens ‚Äúby taste‚Äù, I reframed the UI around task priority and decision clarity. The strategy focused on three pillars:

  1. Task-first hierarchy ‚Üí design the home around what users need to do (not what the brand wants to show).
  2. Clarity of actions ‚Üí explicit CTAs and predictable next steps, with supportive microcopy.
  3. System consistency ‚Üí reusable typography, spacing and components to scale without drift.
KEY DESIGN MOVES

Here are the most impactful design decisions mapped to the problems found:

  1. Explicit logged-in state: added a clear welcome header to confirm status and personalize the experience.
  2. Primary CTA clarity: separated ‚ÄúContinue search‚Äù and ‚ÄúNew search‚Äù to restore user control.
  3. Information hierarchy: reduced competing blocks; grouped content into clear categories.
  4. Progress + feedback: introduced clearer flow steps for ‚ÄúAdd reservation‚Äù and reduced form ambiguity.
  5. Contextual help: added short descriptions where users typically hesitate (documents, baggage, alerts).
VISUAL SYSTEM

To prevent inconsistencies and make the redesign scalable, I created lightweight UI guidelines: spacing scale, typography hierarchy, iconography and a refined blue palette aligned with the brand.

  1. Typography: consistent hierarchy for headings/body to improve scanability.
  2. Spacing: 8/16/24/32px rhythm for predictable layouts.
  3. Icons: unified stroke and style for navigation and actions.
  4. Color: structured blues for states, emphasis, and readability.

OUTCOME & NEXT STEPS

This redesign focuses on clarity and trust: fewer competing elements, more explicit actions, and a scalable system. If I continued the project, I would validate improvements with usability testing and measurable product metrics: task completion rate (Add reservation), time on task, and drop-offs on key flows.

Key learning: In complex products (travel, banking, fintech), UX issues are often structural ‚Äî hierarchy, system feedback and clarity matter as much as visual polish.

Lumix ‚Äî Designing financial clarity through emotional regulation

A behavioral fintech concept that integrates real financial data with emotional support to reduce anxiety and increase perceived control among young professionals.

4 teammates Fintech / Wellness Product Design App 10 days
THE CHALLENGE

Many young professionals with stable income still experience persistent financial stress. Traditional financial apps often amplify anxiety through dense dashboards and data-heavy interfaces.

Our research showed that financial stress is less about numbers and more about perceived lack of control.

The opportunity was clear: Design a financial experience that prioritizes clarity and emotional regulation over feature density.

FINANCIAL CLARITY

Instead of overwhelming users with complex graphs, Lumix provides a simplified monthly overview and focused savings tracking.

Key principles:

  1. One active savings goal to reduce decision fatigue.
  2. Clear progress visualization with predictive guidance.
  3. Reduced visual noise and structured hierarchy.
EMOTIONAL TRACKING

Financial decisions are deeply emotional. Lumix integrates daily mood tracking to identify patterns between spending behavior and emotional states.

This transforms the product from a budgeting tool into a behavioral awareness system.

EDUCATION & GUIDANCE

Instead of passive dashboards, Lumix includes an educational section designed to build financial literacy in a non-intimidating way.

Content focuses on practical micro-habits, mindset shifts and long-term clarity.

CONVERSATIONAL SUPPORT

A conversational assistant translates financial data into actionable insights and empathetic feedback.

The goal is to reduce avoidance behavior and create a safe interaction layer when facing financial data.

MY ROLE

I led research synthesis, defined behavioral opportunity areas and structured the MVP experience. I designed core interaction flows and contributed to visual hierarchy decisions to reduce cognitive load.

KEY LEARNINGS

Financial products do not only communicate data ‚Äî they communicate safety.

Simplicity increases perceived control. Emotional tone influences behavioral consistency. Reducing cognitive load can directly impact trust.
